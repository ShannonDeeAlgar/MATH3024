Modelling#

All of science relies on models.

A model allows us to reason logically from assumptions about a systems structure to conclusions about what we should see, would see under certain conditions, or will see if we intervene.

We turn to models when the part of the world were interested in is difficult to work with directly. For example, the system might be:

  • too large or too small

  • too expensive to test

  • too hard to manipulate

  • or simply too messy to make sense of all at once

Much of the art of the scientist lies in formulating, manipulating, and revising models all with the understanding that the model does not, and should not, attempt to capture the full richness of the world. We dont want an exact replica of the system - we already have that, which is why we need a model! (Shameless plug: My TedX talk)

Modelling in general (and modelling of complex systems in particular) requires:

  • a growing comfort with approximation

  • the use of partial descriptions

  • an acceptance of generalisations with limited scope

These concessions arent weaknesses theyre what make modelling possible, and more importantly, they are what make modelling useful!

Our model models#

We will introduce most of the ideas today via two different observations and the models that were proposed to investigate them:

  1. Planetary orbits are elliptical

  2. Parts of society are segregated

Planetary orbits#

Keplers observation: The orbit of every planet is an ellipse with the Sun at one of the two foci. (This, and his other two laws, were found by analysing astronomical data.)

The model: Isaac Newton proved Kepler was right by following a sort of recipe

  1. he appealed to a general principle - the law of gravitation, which can be

  2. written as a differential equation and

  3. analytically solved this to give an elliptic solution

  4. QED.

This mathematical derivation gives rigor and a sense of absolute and unwavering proof of the observed phenomena, so people tended to like this.

Pause and consider:
(a) Is it perfect?
(b) Can a model be true or false?

(a) No, it isnt perfect or at least it isnt definitely isnt exact. It makes simplifying assumptions and approximations. For example, ignoring smaller influences, like the gravitational forces from distant planets, or relativistic effects these are considered negligible compared to the dominant forces (e.g. Sun-planet interactions). Planets are also treated as point masses, rather than accounting for their actual size, which is small relative to the vast distances between them.

These simplifications are justified because they capture the dominant dynamics with good accuracy, and the omitted factors have only minor effects in most practical cases. So while the model isnt exact, it is reasonable and highly effective.

(b) This is hard to say, we are probably better off speaking in terms of a model being more or less accurate/appropriate/useful. There can be multiple valid models of the same system, each capturing different aspects or working at different levels of approximation. One model might be simpler and easier to work with, while another includes more detail but is harder to analyse. The key is whether the model gives reliable insights for the questions being asked.

Complex Systems (the field)#

aka called Complexity Science. The interdisciplinary study of how interactions give rise to emergent behaviour in systems across nature, society, and technology.

This is essentially a diverse and adaptable toolkit for dealing with complex systems.

Each of these could be a unit on their own, indeed Networks and Nonlinear Dynamics are the focus of our units MATH3002 and MATH3021 respectively. We will not go into much depth on these units but I will make connections to them where relevant

Complex Systems 'text book':
Fig: Sayama's text book, Introduction to the Modeling and Analysis of Complex Systems. It isn't all relevant but it is all freely available online and comes with some Python code too

The study of complex systems traces back to four foundational scientific developments of the 19th century. These historical prequels laid the groundwork for our modern understanding of systems composed of many interacting parts, particularly where the behaviour of the whole cannot be easily deduced from the parts.

  1. Entropy and Thermodynamics
    Key contributors: Carnot, Clausius, Maxwell, Boltzmann, Gibbs
    From the second law of thermodynamics to statistical mechanics, these pioneers helped us understand the delicate interplay between order and disorder, and the constraints they impose on the production of work in physical systems.

  2. Evolution and Adaptation
    Key contributors: Darwin, Wallace, Mendel
    With natural selection and genetic inheritance, biology revealed itself to be a system of adaptive feedback, driven by variation, competition, and replication. The mathematics of population dynamics and evolutionary game theory build on this legacy.

  3. Nonlinear Dynamics
    Key contributors: Maxwell, Poincar
    The study of nonlinear differential equations, stability, and chaos emerged as tools for understanding how deterministic systems can produce unpredictable behaviour from weather to cardiac rhythms.

  4. Computation and Logic
    Key contributors: Babbage, Lovelace, Boole
    The groundwork for algorithmic thinking, symbolic logic, and digital computation was laid. These ideas are now central to understanding the information processing and decision-making capabilities of complex systems.

These pillars still underpin the modern mathematical language of complexity. In addition to them, the 20th century brought conceptual advances from:

  • Cybernetics (feedback, control)

  • General Systems Theory

  • Game Theory (strategic interaction)

As a relatively new field the boundaries and key terms are hotly debated. Personally, I like Hiroki Sayamas topical clusters. These are often overlapping perspectives that reflect the core themes, tools, and phenomena in Complex Systems:

Classical modelling is highly effective at describing systems at two opposite extremes:

  • Simple systems
    These involve a small number of interacting components governed by well-understood rules. Their behaviour is highly predictable and can be described using exact mathematical equations. Example: The motion of planets is accurately modelled by Newtons laws of motion and gravitation.

  • Complicated (but disorganised) systems
    These consist of many components, but interactions are weak, random, or statistically uniform. Their complexity arises from scale, not structure, and they are amenable to averaging and aggregation. Example: A gas made up of billions of atoms can be described by statistical mechanics, which ignores individual behaviour in favour of macro-level regularities.

In both cases, classical systems are typically: closed, reversible, symmetry-dominated, and predictable. They lend themselves to reductionist approaches, where behaviour can be derived from first principles.

At one extreme, we find systems whose behaviour is determined by fundamental symmetries and universal laws, requiring only a few initial conditions. At the other, the system is so complicated that individual details become irrelevant patterns emerge only at the aggregate level, and these can be captured through statistical or probabilistic descriptions.

But classical modelling can struggle when attempting to disentangle complex systems sitting in the awkward space between these extremes and thats exactly where most of the interesting stuff lies!

Complex systems (the systems)#

The actual systems themselves made up of many interacting parts that influence one another in non-trivial ways.

These interactions can produce emergent behaviour patterns, structures, or dynamics not present in any individual component.

For example, bird flocks exhibit coordinated motion that seems almost choreographed turning, twisting, and dispersing as one. But there is no leader and no global control mechanism. Each bird follows a few simple local rules: maintain distance, align with neighbours, and avoid collisions. The flocks elegant motion emerges from these local interactions, i.e. it is a property of the whole, not found in any single bird or rule.

Emergence is one of the key features of a complex system. There are many others listed below. A complex system is likely not exhibit all of these features, but many will share a recognisable subset of traits that serve as signposts that were dealing with a system whose behaviour cannot be understood by studying parts in isolation.

Key features of complex systems#

  • Interactions: across spatial and/or temporal scales.

  • A large number of components (but not too large)

  • Nonlinearity: Relationships between inputs and outputs, or between components within the system, are complex and often not linear.

  • Heterogeneity: Diversity across the components in the system such as different attributes, behaviours, or states.

  • Self-organising: with rules that amplify fluctuations into coordinate space-time patterns

  • Emergence: A theory of the system at some scale or level of organisation is qualitatively different from the lower level scale. i.e the whole is more than the sum of its parts - Aristole. Society is more than a collection of individuals, consciousness is more than a collection of neurons, traffic is more than a collection of cars. This is a behavioural phenomena at the system level

  • Multiscale dynamics: Dynamics occur across multiple scales. The system often lacks of a characteristic scale. How do we go from the dynamics of neurons to brain function and behaviour?

  • Far from equilibrium: complex systems usually exist in an open environment from which individual components can both consume and dissipate energy, allowing for time reversal symmetry to break. Some complex systems are referred to as active matter, if the individuals in the matter are moving somehow then they may be referred to as self-propelled particles. Individuals may also have an ability to express a strategy, adapting, coordinating, optimising as they make decisions in an evolving environment.

    • As a related but somewhat rabbit-holey tangential: Does free-will violate physics?

  • Strong transitions: Abrupt shifts can occur as systems pass critical points (called bifurcation or tipping points). This may continue to produce a cascade, where one failure (or success) can lead to cascading failures (or successes) that may have catastrophic (or beneficial) consequences.

  • Memory: The processes that make up the world are dynamic: the past informs on the future and individual moments cannot be assumed to be decoupled from the history that lead up to that point. Memory in a complex system isnt stored in a single location it is distributed across the system. As a result, the system has a history, and that history plays a crucial role in shaping its behaviour.

  • Feedback Loops: Both negative (dampening), and positive (amplifying) feedback are almost always found in complex systems. Negative feedback tends to move a system towards equilibria that are stable, predictable, and resistant to change, whilst positive feedback moves a system away from equilibria and promotes further disruption. Whether a feedback is classified as positive or negative is based on its influence on the patterns and dynamics.

  • Dissipative: dynamics that are irreversible and break time-reversal symmetry

  • Adaptive: capture of information by a system from the environment that promotes persistence and increases multiplicity

  • More traits:

    • path dependence

    • power laws

    • universality

    • rich phase diagrams

    • produce and destroy diversity

    • inherently prone to collapse

    • resilient

    • stochastic

    • multilayer networks

    • evolution and co-evolution

Complex Systems Thinkers see things differently

Fig: An Introduction to Complex Systems Science and Its Applications by A F. Siegenfeld, [2020])

Most fields tend to focus on the components. They would group each of these systems by columns. Complex Systems emphasises the interactions that occur between these components and complex systems thinkers would group them by row.

This is why Complex Systems is inherently cross-disciplinary! The study of complexity is not defined by a subject or specific system of research, but a way to approach the understanding of systems behaviour.

So whether it is a flock of birds, the particles in a gas, the bacteria in a pond or the people in a crowd we approach modelling and studying them in the same way.

Purpose#

A model should be tailored to address the specific research questions or hypotheses. \(\rightarrow\) Schelling asked how high the neighbour similarity ratio threshold had to be in order for segregation to occur

There are two options to build a model:

  1. using model assumptions that are derived from empirically observed phenomena, and then produce previously unknown collective behaviors by simulation

    • (micro-known to produce macro-unknown)

  2. using hypothetical model assumptions, and then reproduce empirically observed collective phenomena by simulation

    • (micro-unknown to reproduce macro-known)

Pause and consider:
Which approach did Schelling use for his model of segregation?

Schelling model used approach 2: segregation at the population-level was observed in Chicago and the hypothesis that individuals were not being intentionally racist/exclusionary was tested.

Assumptions#

Occams razor: the principle of parsimony.

Entities must not be multiplied beyond necessity.

All thing being equal, the simplest explanation is the best one.
William of Ockham (English philosopher)

Simplicity is a priority!! (at least first)

Dont get tempted to do so much that we cant unpack the whys. e.g.:

  • Dont use two parameters if one will do.

  • Dont look at three-dimensions until you have mastered one- and two-dimensions

Keeping things simple requires assumptions.

Without them (good) modelling is not be feasible.

Assumptions abstract away unnecessary details that may not significantly affect the behaviour of the system being modelled. This helps reveal the underlying patterns and dynamics that are essential for understanding the systems behaviour and making predictions

Assumptions help to:

  • manage inevitable uncertainties, allowing for sensitivity analyses and exploration of different scenarios

  • generalise a model such that it is more broadly applicable and can provide insights across different contexts

  • ensure model behaviour is interpretable

The key to making good assumptions is to be transparent about them and to understand their impact.

Pause and consider:
How did Schelling keep things simple?

What assumptions did he make?

The Schelling model for segregation is very simple, and yet, its highly complex!

Keeping things simple: segregation is obviously a very complex problem but the model used to explore how segregation could emerge was very simple. He analysed worlds that were one- and then eventually two- dimensional grids with simple rules enabling individuals to move if they were unhappy with their neighbours. No complex psychology or social dynamics just local preferences and movement.

Assumptions: Individuals have only mild preferences for similarity. Only 2 types of people were considered. They acted independently with on single reason for movement based on their local conditions with no external forces or coordination.

What goes in to a model?#

There are laws of physics and there are rules of life and rules are meant to be broken.
David Krakauer, Foundational Papers in Complexity Science, Vol. 1, p. 6

  • Independent variable: usually time as we tend to think about systems evolving in time (even if there are multiple independent variables as with wave propagation and heat diffusion). This is our way of thinking about causality a systems state depends on its previous states in time. Time can be discrete, or aligned with events

  • Structure: rules of the system. Format of the rules can be sentences, algorithms, equations ((O, S, P)DEs, difference equations)

  • Data: serves as an input to the rules. Could be as simple as an initial condition but could be continuously interacting. Real or contrived

  • Inputs: parameters, degrees of freedom

  • Dynamics: the rules of the game

What comes out of a model?#

  • Outputs: qualitative and quantitative analysis in the form of various visualisations/abstractions/representations of the resulting behaviour

Modelling is a dynamic, interactive process that allows us to explore a system from multiple perspectives. True insight comes from interacting with a model not just analysing its output.

Model details: The original Parable of the Polygons#

Lets now be more specific about exactly what design decisions were made in the Parable of the Polygons model:

  • World: square grid of size \(20\times 20\) with fixed boundary

  • Individuals/agents: some individuals endowed with a state

  • State:

    • of agent: yellow or blue and grid location.

    • of grid site: occupied or empty

  • Initialisation: random configuration but we arent sure exactly how state is assigned. In two rounds of counting the number of each: \([N_e, N_y, N_b]=[61, 157, 182], [80, 153, 167]\). This is a good lesson on reproducibility!

  • Neighbourhood: occupied sites in Moore neighbourhood, not including self.

  • Dynamics: I wanna move if less than 1/3 of my neighbors are like me. I get to move if randomly selected to do so. My move is to a random unoccupied location.

  • Analysis: Segregation evolving in time but exact definition wasnt provided (grrrr: this is bad even for sci-comms).

Dynamics#

  • Neighbourhood: Moore, not including self. Entirely summarised by similarity ratio \(\frac{n_s}{n}\), where \(n_s\) are the number of similar neighbours in the neighbourhood and \(n = \{1,\ldots,8\}\) is the number of total neighbours

  • Dynamics: Based on local neighbourhood similarity ratio and a threshold \(\theta_r=50\%\). Agent is unhappy and will want to move if \(\frac{n_s}{n}<\theta_r\). Randomly selected unhappy agent will move to random empty location

def find_dissatisfied_indices(grid, threshold_percentage=50):
    """Returns (i, j) coordinates of agents dissatisfied with their neighbourhood."""
    dissatisfied = []
    rows, cols = grid.shape

    for i in range(rows):
        for j in range(cols):
            if grid[i, j] == 0:
                continue  # Skip empty cells

            # Extract 3x3 neighbourhood with bounds checking
            neighbourhood = grid[max(0, i - 1):min(rows, i + 2),
                                 max(0, j - 1):min(cols, j + 2)]

            # Count similar and empty neighbours
            total_neighbours = np.count_nonzero(neighbourhood) - 1  # exclude self
            similar_neighbours = np.count_nonzero(neighbourhood == grid[i, j]) - 1

            if total_neighbours == 0:
                local_mix = 0
            else:
                local_mix = 100 * similar_neighbours / total_neighbours

            if local_mix < threshold_percentage:
                dissatisfied.append((i, j))

    return dissatisfied

def plot_grid_with_dissatisfied(grid, threshold_percentage=50, title=None, ax=None, show_legend=True, find_dissatisfied_indices=None):
    """
    Plot the Schelling grid with dissatisfied agents marked.

    Parameters:
    - grid: 2D numpy array
    - threshold_percentage: dissatisfaction threshold
    - title: optional title string
    - ax: matplotlib axis (optional)
    - show_legend: whether to display legend
    - find_dissatisfied_indices: function to identify dissatisfied agents (must return (i, j) tuples)
    """
    assert find_dissatisfied_indices is not None, "Must provide find_dissatisfied_indices function"

    cmap = ListedColormap(["white", "#FFD700", "#4682B4"])

    if ax is None:
        fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(4, 4))

    ax.imshow(grid, cmap=cmap, vmin=0, vmax=2)
    ax.set_xticks([]); ax.set_yticks([])

        ax.set_title(title, pad=12)

def run_one_simulation(grid, find_dissatisfied_indices=None, get_mean_similarity=None, threshold_percentage=50,
                       force_iterate=False, collect_data=False, collect_frames=False, sim_rng=None, max_iterations=200):
    """Simulates Schelling model.

    Options:
    - collect_data: returns MSR and dissatisfaction over time
    - collect_frames: returns frames and moves for animation

    Returns:
    - If both False: final grid only
    - If collect_data: (msr, dissatisfaction_counts, grid)
    - If collect_frames: (dissatisfaction_counts, grid, frames, moves)
    - If both: (msr, dissatisfaction_counts, grid, frames, moves)
    """
    assert find_dissatisfied_indices is not None, "Must provide a dissatisfaction function"

    # Ensure get_mean_similarity is passed if needed
    if collect_data and get_mean_similarity is None:
        raise ValueError("Must provide get_mean_similarity when collect_data=True")

    # Otherwise provide dummy if not needed
    if get_mean_similarity is None:
        def get_mean_similarity(grid): return None
            
    if sim_rng is None:
        sim_rng = default_rng()
        print("WARNING: new random seed generated")
            
    grid = grid.copy()
    iterations = 0
    prev_grid = grid.copy()

    msr = [get_mean_similarity(grid)] if collect_data else None
    dissatisfaction_counts = [len(find_dissatisfied_indices(grid, threshold_percentage))] if (collect_data or collect_frames) else None
    frames = [grid.copy()] if collect_frames else None
    moves = [None] if collect_frames else None

    while iterations < max_iterations:
        dissatisfied = find_dissatisfied_indices(grid, threshold_percentage)

        if not dissatisfied:
            if not force_iterate:
                break
            # No move  just continue collecting static data
            iterations += 1
            if collect_data:
                msr.append(get_mean_similarity(grid))
            if dissatisfaction_counts is not None:
                dissatisfaction_counts.append(0)
            if collect_frames:
                frames.append(grid.copy())
                moves.append(None)
            continue

        # Pick a dissatisfied agent (i, j) directly
        i, j = sim_rng.choice(dissatisfied)

        empties = np.argwhere(grid == 0)
        if empties.size == 0:
            break

        m, n = empties[sim_rng.integers(len(empties))]
        grid[m, n], grid[i, j] = grid[i, j], 0

        iterations += 1

        if collect_data:
            msr.append(get_mean_similarity(grid))
        if dissatisfaction_counts is not None:
            dissatisfaction_counts.append(len(find_dissatisfied_indices(grid, threshold_percentage)))
        if collect_frames:
            frames.append(grid.copy())
            moves.append(((i, j), (m, n)))

        if not force_iterate and np.array_equal(prev_grid, grid):
            break
        prev_grid = grid.copy()

    # Return based on collection options
    if collect_data and collect_frames:
        return msr, dissatisfaction_counts, grid, frames, moves
    elif collect_data:
        return msr, dissatisfaction_counts, grid
    elif collect_frames:
        return grid, frames, moves
    else:
        return grid

Pause and consider:
What do you expect a good measure of segregation would give for each of these scenarios?

What properties should a good measure of the distribution of individuals capture?

The example grids have varied combinations of these five distributional characteristics (or other dimensions depending on how you define them):

  • evenness: how a given population group is spread, maximised when the local distribution reflects the global distribution

  • exposure: the amount of contact that occurs with other population groups

  • clustering: the number of groups of a given population

  • concentration: captures the physical space occupied

  • centralisation: around an urban core.

Massey and Denton (1988) for a systematic evaluation of 20 measures

The dimensions capture different facets of segregation, each with different social and behavioural implications. And of course there are many overlaps e.g. a centralised group is likely to have a high clustering.

The best measure or index for segregation has been hotly debated and the reality is that segregation should probably be measured with several indices simultaneously. Maybe this is why the Parable of the Polygons doesnt tell us exactly how they calculate segregation!

One option for quantifying segregation#

The mean similarity ratio:

\[ \mathcal{MSR}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_i^N \frac{n_s}{n}, \]

where \(n_s\), \(n_e\), \(n\) denote the number of similar, empty and total neighbours in a Moore neighbourhood of the individual \(i\). Hence, \(n=9-1-n_e\)

This is what we will choose (and what Schelling used). But there are many other choices.

In theory, the expected MSR for a perfectly mixed, infinite two-group grid is 0.5. In practice, smaller values can occur on finite grids (or with uneven populations).

Wed prefer our measure varied between 0 and 1, where 0 indicates a completely integrated and diverse population and 1 indicates complete segregation, because its more directly comparable to other measures and easier to interpret (can normalise).

def get_mean_similarity(grid):
    """Average similarity ratio across all non-empty agents."""
    ratios, count = 0.0, 0

    rows, cols = grid.shape
    for idx in range(rows * cols):
        i, j = divmod(idx, cols)

        if grid[i, j] == 0:
            continue
        neighbourhood = grid[max(0, i-1):min(grid_size, i+2),
                             max(0, j-1):min(grid_size, j+2)]
        empty_n   = np.count_nonzero(neighbourhood == 0)
        tot_neigh = neighbourhood.size - empty_n - 1
        if tot_neigh > 0:
            similar = np.count_nonzero(neighbourhood == grid[i, j]) - 1
            ratios += similar / tot_neigh
            count  += 1
    return ratios / max(count, 1)

#time to steady state
def compute_time_to_steady_state(msr, epsilon=1e-3, window=5, max_iterations=200):
    diffs = np.abs(np.diff(msr))
    for i in range(len(diffs) - window):
        if np.all(diffs[i:i+window] < epsilon):
            return i + window
    return max_iterations

epsilon = 1e-3
window = 5

def run_msr_ensemble(grid_size, state_probs, thresholds=None, seeds=range(5),
                     run_simulation_func=None, find_dissatisfied_indices=None,
                     get_mean_similarity=None):
    """
    Run an ensemble of Schelling model simulations across multiple thresholds.
    """
    if thresholds is None:
        thresholds = list(range(10, 100, 5))

    msr_ensemble_results = {}

    for threshold in tqdm(thresholds, desc="Thresholds"):
        all_runs = []
        for seed in tqdm(seeds, desc=f"Seed runs (T={threshold})", leave=False):
            init_rng = default_rng(seed)
            sim_rng = default_rng(seed)

            grid = init_rng.choice([0, 1, 2], (grid_size, grid_size), p=state_probs)

            msr, *_ = run_simulation_func(
                grid=grid,
                find_dissatisfied_indices=find_dissatisfied_indices,
                get_mean_similarity=get_mean_similarity,
                threshold_percentage=threshold,
                collect_data=True,
                collect_frames=False,
                force_iterate=True,
                sim_rng=sim_rng
            )
            all_runs.append(msr)
        
        msr_ensemble_results[threshold] = all_runs

    return msr_ensemble_results

How could we continue to improve our understanding?#

  1. A larger grid?: More moves will be required to reach a stable configuration. Can we get a feel for how many moves before we do a whole lot of computation that is insufficient/excessive?

  • Plot of threshold vs mean time to steady state for various grid sizes

  • The maximum number of iterations could be a function of the threshold

  1. More thresholds?: Theyre not all equally interesting.

  • We can do a finer graining of the threshold around the transitions

  1. Further experimenting:

  • More agent types

  • Noisy options for movement

  • Heterogeneity in threshold

  • Underlying topology of map

  1. Alternative quantities that capture the behaviour. The \(\mathcal{MSR}\) we defined is the average of an individual-level metric. There are many others we could cook up such as the average number of moves individuals have to make to become happy or the fraction of unhappy individuals in a population. We could also look at group-level measures such as cluster size distributions or entropy (more on entropy in Week 9).

Mathematics is the language of complex systems

Mathematics is the language with which God has written the universe.
Galileo Galilei
The unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics in the natural sciences.
Eugene Wigner

Its often assumed that mathematics can only handle the simplest cases, e.g. linear systems, ideal gases, frictionless planes. But this view is outdated.

Mathematics simplifies complex systems by abstracting away extraneous detail, making them tractable through idealisation and model building. It provides the foundational toolsprobability, differential equations, information theory, networksfor quantitative reasoning and clear, testable insights into the principles that drive system behaviour.

Despite the complexity of biological, economic, and social systems, mathematics remains our most precise and powerful language for describing how systems behave and for making testable predictions about them.

The complex domain encompasses the world of self-organising and evolving agents at all scales: from organisms to societies and mathematics offers a universal framework that reveals deep structural similarities between seemingly unrelated systems. We will see throughout this unit that the same model can be used to explore the dynamics of neurons, cities, markets, and flocks of birds alike.

We are now in the midst of a new era

In both science and philosophy, a paradigm refers to a unique framework consisting of concepts, thought patterns, theories, research methods, postulates, and criteria that define what is considered valid and valuable within a particular field of study

Complex Systems is a relatively new field (coined circa 1980 with birth of Santa Fe Institute). It is new in that the tools, the thinking and the criteria for judging a model are new. It is at the centre of one of the most radical new scientific paradigms of the twentieth century, whih has seen a shift in what we consider to be (good) science and good modelling.

With Complex Systems, the way we do science has changed forever:

  • emergence over reductionism

  • transition from a traditional analytical description of nature (used with tremendous success since Galileo and Newton) towards an algorithmic description (perhaps in parallel with a data-driven/AI description)

  • learning is being done across disciplines with common and shared methodologies dissolving boundaries

  • 2021 Nobel prize was awarded to Complex Systems Scientists for groundbreaking contributions to our understanding of complex systems (Syukuro Manabe and Klaus Hasselmann (Earth climate), Giorgio Parisi (disordered and random processes)).

  • massive data collection and computing power are available

  • social relevance (and in some cases urgency) of problems is prompting further work

  • mainstream social understanding of complex systems and the Complex Systems approach is improving (slowly)

  • journals dedicated to Complex Systems

The biggest challenges of the 21st century are 'complex'

These challenges are:

  • tied together by a common theme: emergent complexity in highly-interconnected systems

  • at the boundary of theory and the unknown

  • not so unknown that theyre hopeless, but theres not enough theory to predict the results of our decisions.

Find one that excites you and build your project around it.
