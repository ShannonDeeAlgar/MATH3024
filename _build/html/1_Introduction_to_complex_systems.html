Welcome to MATH3024: Complex Systems#

This unit (re)integrates play with learning

It reflects Complex Systems generally in that:

  • multidisciplinary

  • intuitive and accessible examples

  • mathematics is the language used to describe and analyse systems

  • code is unavoidable and welcomed, allowing exploration and manipulation of systems

  • cutting edge research will feature throughout.

It will likely be your first foray into research (and research integrated learning/teaching). It may also be your first experience with coding. These are essential skills for anyone working with complex systems.

Why study Complex Systems at UWA?#

  • we are a research-intensive university

  • and we have particular strength in Complex Systems!

  • members of our group have been instrumental in developing key parts of the field and are world-renowned for their work.

Assessment#

Learning outcomes#

In general at university we are aiming to prepare you for successful careers, active citizenship, and lifelong learning. In this unit we want you to become a Complex Systems Thinker

Complex Systems Thinkers have a holistic and integrative approach to problem-solving. They can identify and understand the interconnections and interdependencies within complex systems

The specific learning outcomes that you can look forward to are:

  • Understanding: fundamental concepts, principles, and characteristics of complex systems

  • Modelling: employ various modelling techniques, tools and methods to describe complex systems

  • Analysis: employ mathematical methods/visualisation techniques to analyse complex systems

  • Application: apply Complex Systems theory for understanding of real systems and data

  • Communication: effectively communicate ideas, models, and findings related to complex systems through written reports, appropriate data analysis and visualisation

  • Research: read and understand scientific research papers.

Ultimately, all of this aims at developing an intuition of complex systems that will guide decision-making.

Yes, this is different…#

This is, first and foremost, a self-directed third year research project.

The task is intentionally ill-defined to allow you an opportunity to engage in research and assessment in a way that is relevant to you, your interests and your future path, whilst developing practical skills relevant to Complex Systems

You are expected to take the initiative for your project and ultimately how much work you do is up to you. Projects will be judged on extent and quality with highest marks awarded for implementation and analysis that goes beyond what has already been done. To get there, you will need to start small and build up your understanding of the system and your model by increasing the complexity. Do not leave it to the last minute! I am happy to discuss your ideas and results at any stage throughout the semester, but do not expect a step-by-step outline of what to do (it simply doesn’t exist).

Note that the Project is released in the first week to allow you to mull over ideas as we learn new things, not to cause you to panic if you do not have a project well defined by the end of week 3!

Expectations surrounding ChatGPT etc#

My view: We are ‘raising you’ to be critical thinkers in a tech-centred world. ChatGPT is like a calculator - and I’m not teaching you to do addition. I’m not teaching you to code either, so if it frees up cognitive resources for you to think deeply about a problem… go for it

From an ethical standpoint you should declare how you have used ChatGPT. e.g. ‘The human generated content was edited and condensed by ChatGPT + Version’. ‘ChatGPT + Version partially/fully wrote this code with \(N\) iterations of prompts’ (consider attaching your conversations and definitely keep them for integrity checks)

A warning: ChatGPT can say some really dumb things and it is very easily confused, convinced of wrong things and overly confident in non-factual responses. You must ask good questions, defend good questions, and fact check – skills I am very happy for you to practise. As critical thinkers, you have a responsibility to read and evaluate the content before incorporating it into your work and associating it with your name. Ensure that any work you submit genuinely reflects your own viewpoint and that you aren’t blindly copying someone/something else’s views because if you do this you can expect to be heavily penalised.

Our ‘model’ models#

We will introduce most of the ideas today via two different observations and the models that were proposed to investigate them:

  1. Planetary orbits are elliptical

  2. Parts of society are segregated

Planetary orbits#

Kepler’s observation: The orbit of every planet is an ellipse with the Sun at one of the two foci. (This, and his other two laws, were found by analysing astronomical data.)

The model: Isaac Newton proved Kepler was right by following a sort of ‘recipe’…

  1. he appealed to a general principle - the law of gravitation, which can be

  2. written as a differential equation and

  3. analytically solved this to give an elliptic solution

  4. QED.

This mathematical derivation gives rigor and a sense of absolute and unwavering proof of the observed phenomena, so people tended to like this

Is it perfect?

No - ignores some stuff: forces of say other planets or relativistic effects, which we justify because they are small relative to those we deem important, size of planet, modelled as point masses as actual size is negligible relative to inter-planet distance. But these assumptions/approximations seem reasonable and justified.

Background: America is set to be a ‘majority-minority nation’ by 2044

Colouring a map of a city like Chicago based on the racial profile of it’s people, what do you expect to see?

Schelling’s observation: Segregation

How are our two models different?#

Elliptic orbits vs segregation model:

  • equation-based vs. rule-based

  • analytic solution vs. numerical solution via computational integration i.e. analysis vs. simulation

  • simplicity (e.g. continuous, linear, reductionism, equilibrium, independence, deterministic, abstract, few particles, homogenous) vs complexity (discrete, nonlinear, non-equilibrium, interaction, detailed, many particles and heterogenous)

  • physical vs. social (free-will?)

How are our two models the same?#

Both models:

  • seek insight into a physical observation

  • make assumptions

  • kept things simple

  • have parameters

  • made generalisable conclusions

Is one model better than the other?

Complex Systems as a field#

Complex Systems is essentially a diverse and adaptable toolkit for dealing with complex systems

Note that I will typically capitalise the discipline and use lower case for a system that is complex.

‘Classical’ modelling describes the extreme scenarios well…

  • Simple systems: comprised of a small number of interacting components that can be easily described using mathematical models.

    • e.g. motion of planets

    • described well by classical mechanics

  • Complicated (disorganised) systems: comprised of a large number of mostly non-interacting components and are amenable to aggregation and linear modelling.

    • e.g. The billions of atoms in a gas, but their interactions are trivial

    • statistical mechanics describes their macro-scale behaviour well

These classical systems are normally closed, reversible, symmetry-dominated and predictable and we have very good theory to describe these systems and their behaviour

On the other hand, classical modelling can struggle when attempting to disentangle complex systems sitting between these extremes.

… and that’s exactly where most of the interesting stuff lies!

Organised complex systems: are composed of large numbers of parts (as in the disorganised systems) with non-trivial (typically nonlinear) interactions spanning multiple scales

They are characterised by properties such as…

  • open (positioned within some gradient, e.g. chemical)

  • self-organising (with rules that amplify fluctuations into coordinate space-time patterns)

  • dissipative (dynamics that are irreversible and break time-reversal symmetry)

  • uncertain (open-ended space of possible states not enumerable in initial conditions)

  • adaptive (capture of information by a system from the environment that promostes persistence and increases multiplicity)

At one extreme there are fundamental regularities described using minimal assumptions, simple rules and few initial conditions: symmetry and law

At the other there are emergent regularities constructed from contingent histories described with coarse-grained rules and nested boundary conditions: information and adaptation

`There are laws of physics and there are rules of life – and rules are meant to be broken’ - Volume 1 page 6

The complex domain encompasses the world of self-organising and evolving agents at all scales: from organisms to societies

… very diverse! Can a single framework really study them all?

(figure essentially stolen from Prof. Michael Small (with permission). Pretty brain: Consciousness by Greg Dunn)

Key features of complex systems#

  • Interactions: across spatial and/or temporal scales.

  • A large number of components (but not too large)

  • Nonlinearity: Relationships between inputs and outputs, or between components within the system, are complex and often not linear.

  • Heterogeneity: Diversity across the components in the system such as different attributes, behaviours, or states.

  • Emergence: A theory of the system at some scale or level of organisation is qualitatively different from the lower level scale. i.e the whole is more than the sum of its parts - Aristole. Society is more than a collection of individuals, consciousness is more than a collection of neurons, traffic is more than a collection of cars. This is a behavioural phenomena at the system level

  • Multiscale dynamics: Dynamics occur across multiple scales. The system often lacks of a characteristic scale. How do we go from the dynamics of neurons to brain function and behaviour?

    • Strong transitions: Abrupt shifts can occur as systems pass critical points (called bifurcation or tipping points). This may continue to produce a cascade, where one failure (or success) can lead to cascading failures (or successes) that may have catastrophic (or beneficial) consequences.

    • Memory: The processes that make up the world are dynamic: the past informs on the future and individual moments cannot be assumed to be decoupled from the history that lead up to that point.

    • Feedback Loops: Both negative (dampening), and positive (amplifying) feedback are almost always found in complex systems. Negative feedback tends to move a system towards equilibria that are stable, predictable, and resistant to change, whilst positive feedback moves a system away from equilibria and promotes further disruption. Whether a feedback is classified as positive or negative is based on its influence on the patterns and dynamics.

Complex Systems Thinkers see things differently#

Most fields tend to focus on the components. They would group the below systems by columns

But Complex Systems emphasises the interactions that occur between these components and would group them by row - cross-disciplinary

(figure from An Introduction to Complex Systems Science and Its Applications by A F. Siegenfeld)

The study of complexity is not defined by a subject or specific system of research, but a way to approach the understanding of systems behaviour

Changing paradigm#

In both science and philosophy, a paradigm refers to a unique framework consisting of concepts, thought patterns, theories, research methods, postulates, and criteria that define what is considered valid and valuable within a particular field of study

Complex Systems is a relatively ‘new’ field, being coined circa 1980. It is new in that the tools, the thinking and the criteria for judging a model are new. It is at the centre of a gradual shift in what we consider to be (good) science… and good modelling.

It is one of the most radical new scientific paradigms of the twentieth century, forging four areas of research into a new way of seeing the world: entropy, evolution, dynamics and computation. Complex systems – the theory of far-from-equilibrium, purposeful machines

The sorts of questions we ask of these systems: optimisation, robustness, resilience, stability, energy dissipation, regulatory mechanics, origination, informational constraints, computational costs and limits

We are also making a transition from a traditional analytical description of nature (used with tremendous success since Galileo and Newton) towards an algorithmic description (perhaps in parallel with a data-driven/AI description)

Measuring complexity

At its worst, … equates the many principles of complexity with the search for a universal metric that might apply to all observables: the complexity of a brain, sicieties and technologies, all on one single axis of quantification. This is a case of techniques substituting for theories. It would be like trying to measure the degree of ‘chemistry’ in all chemical reactions or the quotient of ‘anthropology’ in a society. …

There are schools of thought in which everything is mathematics, everything is physics, everything is poetry, and everything is religion. These kinds of framing of reality are lacking in richness, subtlety, modesty, and value. The last thing we want is for everything to be complexity. – Page 11

The four pillars (the prequels of complexity science in from the nineteenth century)

  • Entropy (statistical mechnics, thermodynamics). Work of: Maxwell, Boltzmann, Carnot, Clausius, Gibbs) - principles of order and disorder in relation to the production of mechanical work

  • Evolution and adaptation. Work of: Darwin, Wallace, Mendel - mathematics of the evolutionary and adaptive process

  • Nonlinear dynamcis. Work of: Poincare, Maxwell - regulation, control and prediction of nonlinear dynamical systems

  • Computation and logic (machinery of difference and analytical engines). Work of Babbage, Lovelace, Boole - informational and computational requirements of purposefull or intelligent behaviour

Other prequels/attempts to understand systems that interact in non-trivial ways (text book) Cybernetics, systems theory game theory,

The elements of complex systems are becoming clear: dynamcial multilayer networks, scaling, statistical mechanics of algorithmic dynamics, evolution and co-evolution and information theory - we do not cover networks, dynamics or random processes (there are separate units for each of these)

The role/contribution of mathematics to complex systems#

“Mathematics is the language with which God has written the universe.” - Galileo Galilei

Unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics and its ability to describe physical phenomena - Wigner

It’s often thought that mathematics can only handle simplest of cases but this is simply not true. The maths:

  • makes complex systems simple so as to reduce complexity and tackle things systematically because simple models provide deep insight into fundamental principles!

  • underpins our models (probability, statistics, DEs, networks, …)

Our goal is to model the system to gain a better understanding of it with the hope that we can control the emergent properties of these complex systems.

Complexity comes from the Latin word “plexus”, which indicates non-separability of components

As a relatively new field the boundaries and key terms are hotly debated. Personally, I like Hiroki Sayama’s topical clusters:

  • Collective behaviour

  • Networks

  • Evolution and adaptation

  • Pattern formation

  • Systems theory

  • Nonlinear dynamics

  • Game theory

Each of these could be a unit on their own, indeed Networks and Nonlinear Dynamics are the focus of our units MATH3002 and MATH3021 respectively. We will not go into much depth on these units but I will make connections to them where relevant

The clusters are a combination of modelling tools, general properties and theoretical fields

Sayama’s text book, Introduction to the Modeling and Analysis of Complex Systems is freely available online and comes with some python code too.

The biggest challenges of the 21st century are ‘complex’#

These challenges are:

  • tied together by a common theme: emergent complexity in highly-interconnected systems

  • at the boundary of theory and the unknown

  • not so unknown that they’re hopeless, but there’s not enough theory to predict the results of our decisions.

We are now in the midst of a new era:

  • emergence over reductionism

  • learning is being done across disciplines with common and shared methodologies dissolving boundaries

  • 2021 Nobel prize was awarded to Complex Systems Scientists “for groundbreaking contributions to our understanding of complex systems” (Syukuro Manabe and Klaus Hasselmann (Earth climate), Giorgio Parisi (disordered and random processes)).

  • massive data collection and computing power are available

  • social relevance (and in some cases urgency) of problems is prompting further work

  • mainstream social understanding of complex systems and the Complex Systems approach is improving (slowly)

  • journals dedicated to Complex Systems

“All models are wrong, but some are useful” – George Box.

https://complexity.simplecast.com/episodes/1/transcript…

Yeah. So there’s two issues here. Right, to answer this properly we have to understand what complexity is, and complexity is this domain of reality that straddles the very regular and the random. And science has been really good at those two limits, right? And so one limit, classical mechanics, and the other limit, statistical mechanics. And both are powerful theories, one dealing with, if you like, crystals and the other one dealing with gases. The perfectly ordered and the very disordered. And in the middle is where it all gets complicated and complex, and that’s where we live at SFI.

Now, what that’s done, because science is not very good there, historically, is generated two possible approaches. One of them is complexity science and one of them is machine learning and AI, and they do different things. Machine learning and AI takes all that complexity in, encodes it in big models like deep neural networks, and makes predictions, but those predictions are completely opaque and don’t give anyone an understanding as to how they were reached. On the other hand, you have complexity science, which tries to, in Murray’s language, take “a crude look at the whole.” It tries to find the right scale at which you can do theory of these adaptive systems, if you like, in the center, with a view to not producing perfect predictions, but generating real insight, explanation for why they exist.

And I think we’re now entering in the 21st century, a new kind of scientific schism where we’re going to live with two very different ways of engaging with reality. A machine-based, high-dimensional, very precise predictive framework that is a black box … and ours, which is a more familiar framework from the history of science, if you like, but that is faithful to the complexity of the systems we study, which doesn’t predict so well, but does allow us to understand the basic mechanisms generating the phenomena of interest. And that’s where I think complexity lives, and it’s going to have to come to terms with living with machine learning and AI. It’s almost as if we’ve returned, to use your biblical metaphors, to the Cain and Abel, and those two brothers are going to have to get on as opposed to one killing the other.
